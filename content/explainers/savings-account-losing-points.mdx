# Why a 4% savings account is losing you 7 points a year

A 4% high-yield savings account sounds safe. In nominal dollars, it is. At these assumptions, against a typical Family basket — housing in your zip, childcare, healthcare — it's *losing roughly 7 points a year.*

Safety in nominal dollars is not the same as safety against the life you want.

## The arithmetic

A representative Family basket weighted 30% housing + 25% S&P 500 + 20% childcare + 15% private K–12 tuition + 10% healthcare currently inflates at *roughly* 11.0% per year, 5-year trailing. Components, rounded:

- Housing (national, Case-Shiller 5-yr trailing): *roughly* 9.0%
- S&P 500 (5-yr trailing): *roughly* 10.4%
- Childcare (Care.com + BLS, 5-yr trailing): *roughly* 11.8%
- Private K–12 tuition (College Board): *roughly* 5.0%
- Healthcare (CMS NHEA): *roughly* 5.5%

Your Aspire Rate, at this basket, is somewhere in the high single digits — call it *roughly* 11%. That's the rate your money needs to grow at to keep pace with the cost of the life you're pricing.

A 4% high-yield savings account, against an 11% Aspire Rate, leaves you with an Aspire Gap of *roughly* −7 points. Every year. Compounded.

## What "losing 7 points a year" actually does over time

A $100,000 balance growing at 4% reaches *roughly* $148,000 in 10 years. That's the safe-feeling number.

The same $100,000 *priced against* a basket inflating at 11% needs to reach *roughly* $284,000 in 10 years to keep pace. The 4% account gets you 52% of the way there. The other 48% is the gap that keeps growing.

This is not a story about being smart with markets. It's a story about *which goalpost you're racing.* The savings account beats CPI. CPI is a national basket. CPI is not your life.

## Why this is hard to see

Three reasons.

First, the savings account *number goes up.* Up feels like winning. Most people don't run a parallel calculation against the cost of the life they want.

Second, the loss is invisible until the goal is in arm's reach. You only notice you're 7 points a year behind the year you actually try to buy the house, enroll the kid, or carry the parent.

Third, the framing in personal finance writing favors the safe-vehicle story. *"Beat inflation"* gets used loosely. It usually means *"beat CPI."* CPI ignores most of the basket of things people are saving toward.

## What the Simulator does with this

The Simulator's Allocation lever is the most direct way to see what closes a 7-point Aspire Gap. At these assumptions, shifting from 100% cash to a 70% equities / 30% cash mix typically closes 3–4 points. Going to 100% equities (long-horizon, high-volatility) typically closes another 2 points. The remaining gap requires either a longer timeline, a different basket, a different geography, or higher contributions.

None of those moves is a recommendation. Each is a *visible tradeoff.* That's the point.
